Aligning traces for performance evaluation

Architects commonly use traces containing hardware and software metrics to understand performance. Unfortunately, it is not feasible to collect all metrics in a single trace. For example, modern processors can collect hundreds of distinct hardware events, but they can collect only a few at a time, requiring multiple runs to collect all the metrics. Unfortunately there is enough non-determinism, even in carefully controlled experiments, that we cannot immediately reason across traces from different runs: instead we need to align the traces before we can reason across them. This paper describes and evaluates techniques for aligning traces. Our trace alignment techniques enable performance analysts to safely and effectively reason across traces. We evaluate two different alignment techniques: Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) and Full Uniform Scaling (FUS), and demonstrate that DTW consistently outperforms FUS. To align two traces, DTW requires at least one metric (the alignment metric) common to both traces. We demonstrate that the choice of alignment metric is critical to the performance of DTW. We introduce and evaluate two novel techniques for picking a good alignment metric. Both techniques significantly outperform a carefully, but manually, picked alignment metric. Finally, we describe a novel criteria which indicates a priori whether nor not DTW will yield a good alignment.
